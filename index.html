Galea (ga-le-a) comes from a Latin word meaning “helmet.” It was brought into English as an anatomy term in the 1800s, and today it has a few different meanings:

In botany, the galea is the part of certain flowers that looks like a helmet.

In anatomy, the the galea aponeurotica is a layer of tissue covering the skull.

In medicine, “galea” is a historical term for headaches that cover the entire head, like a helmet.

Headaches are an incredibly common ailment that have been described in some of the earliest medical texts. There are two main types of headaches:

Primary Headaches

Secondary Headaches

In other words, primary headaches are the disease, not the symptom, and secondary headaches are the symptom, not the disease. Both types can be painful and debilitating.

Because the brain itself cannot feel pain, all headache pain comes from blood vessels in the skull, membranes around the brain (meninges), or surrounding nerves.
